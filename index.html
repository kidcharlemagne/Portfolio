Running

Now this is a story all about how my life got flipped/turned upside down. And I'd like to take a minute; just sit right there. I'll tell you how I became...a daily runner. Scroll down to see my activity on Strava!

Having lost my former athletic build to my freshman year diet, I sought to change myself the summer of 2016. Earlier this year, Mark Zuckerberg announced his "run a mile every day for 365 days" challenge, which, to me, sounded like a good start as I had already been weightlifting four to five days a week but I had avoided cardio for years, because of the misguided gym rat mentality that running gets in the way of strength training. At first, I struggled to run a mile in 10 minutes, but after a month I kept trying and was able to do the occasional two mile run, progressing incrementally but cautiously, keeping the streak top priority. After two months, I had completed my first personal 5K. By the time fall semester started, my work load picked up and I reverted back to mostly single mile runs with occasional "long runs" in the mix. I never felt the desire to quit after keeping this up for so long. Bruting the -20 degree wind chill in Blacksburg was far and beyond one of the more difficult parts of this journey, although admittedly, I had to rely on treadmills. Regardless of circumstance (sickness, CS 3214 projects, etc.), I had to run at least a single mile between 12:00 AM to 11:59 PM every day.

On June 5th, 2017, I made the switch from Runkeeper to Strava (donned the "social network" for athletes), porting all the .gpx files into my new account, proudly reviewing my uninterrupted streak. Before I simply tracked my runs as a means for continuity, but having communities of runners and more advanced data tracking gave me more motivation to push my limits. I was no longer satisfied with just a measly mile, so I ramped up to two or more that same day. After a few weeks, I was very comfortable with running a mile and another back and segued into a 5K minimum, which still feels like an arbitrary metric (excuse the bad pun) but sounds a lot more than an extra .10686 miles. I thought I would plateau soon or later since this used to be a "long run" for me. After a month of 5Ks all the way up to the occasional 10K, I felt ready for a new challenge: a 4 mile minimum. On the second day of this new streak, I ran 10 miles for the first time in my life.

Fast forward to the present: winter is coming, harshened by the undeniable presence of global warming. I've been running at least 30 miles every week since mid-August and hope to keep it up even when the weather becomes unforgiving. All the miles I've put in taught me a lot about the a(na)erobic exercise, gave me a second wind, provided an outlet for introspection, and conditioned me to perservere and chase self-actualization like never before. It's refreshing to reminisce how much I've matured as a runner and as a person, but I'll admit running isn't for everyone. It certainly wasn't for me at first, but like most acquired tastes, it gets better with age.
